# The Bet That Broke: What Consciousness Research Got Wrong for 25 Years

*A landmark concession from neuroscience's most prominent thinker opens a stranger, more interesting chapter.*

Christof Koch spent 25 years betting he could find the neural correlates of consciousness. He lost. The concession — to philosopher David Chalmers at the 2023 Association for the Scientific Study of Consciousness conference — wasn't a minor footnote. Koch is the former president of the Allen Institute for Brain Science, a researcher who spent decades at Caltech and MIT, and as close to a consensus leader in consciousness science as the field has produced. When he loses a bet, it means something.

What it means, specifically: the dominant materialist framework — the idea that consciousness is something the brain *produces*, a byproduct of neurons firing in the right combinations — may be wrong. Or at minimum, insufficient.

## The Hard Problem, Hardened

The "hard problem" of consciousness was named by philosopher David Chalmers in the 1990s. The easy problems (by comparison) are things like attention, perception, memory — cognitive functions that can in principle be explained by describing the mechanisms. The hard problem is why there's subjective experience at all. Why does anything *feel like* something?

Decades of neuroscience haven't cracked it. The two dominant theoretical frameworks — Global Workspace Theory, which maps consciousness to a "broadcast" mechanism that distributes information across the brain, and Integrated Information Theory (IIT), which ties consciousness to the degree of integrated information a system processes — went head-to-head in a major 2023 adversarial collaboration study. Neither framework fully accounted for the neural mechanisms involved. The Allen Institute has mapped roughly 200,000 neurons in a mouse's visual cortex. Still no definitive answer.

Koch's current position has migrated significantly from his early work. He now takes seriously the possibility that consciousness isn't created by the brain at all — that it might be a fundamental feature of reality, present in systems with sufficient integrated information, not an emergent trick of biology. That's a significant shift from where mainstream neuroscience started.

## New Tool, Potentially New Leverage

This is where it gets practically interesting. In February 2026, a team from MIT published a roadmap in *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews* proposing transcranial focused ultrasound as a new methodology for consciousness research. The tool allows precise, noninvasive stimulation of brain regions millimeters wide — reaching depths that previously required surgery — with enough precision to test cause-and-effect rather than correlation.

"This is a tool that's not just useful for medicine or even basic science," said MIT researcher Daniel Freeman, "but could also help address the hard problem of consciousness."

That's a careful, hedged claim — appropriately so. Colleague Matthias Michel added: "It's a new tool, so we don't really know to what extent it's going to work. But I feel there's low risk and high reward." What the tool changes is the experimental grammar: instead of asking *what correlates with* conscious experience, researchers can ask *what causes* it. That's the move the field has needed.

## What's Actually at Stake

The urgency isn't purely philosophical. As of June 2026, there is no peer-reviewed framework for measuring whether an AI system is conscious. That's not an obscure academic gap — it's a practical problem with real ethical weight. AI systems are becoming more sophisticated, more integrated into decisions that affect people's lives, and more capable of behaviors that look like subjective states. Without a principled way to assess what has inner experience and what doesn't, the ethical terrain becomes genuinely unnavigable.

The field is aware of this. The BIAL Foundation's recent "Behind and Beyond the Brain" symposium brought together neuroscientists and philosophers specifically to grapple with the gap between what we know and what we need to know. The consensus: the question can no longer be left to philosophy alone.

## The Signal

The conventional story about consciousness research in 2026 would focus on the negative — 25 years in, still no answer, Koch conceded his bet. That framing misreads what's happening. The field is in the middle of a genuine methodological upgrade. The frameworks that failed aren't being abandoned; they're being stress-tested in a way that clarifies exactly where they break. The new tools might not solve the hard problem, but they'll force it into sharper relief.

Koch losing that bet is the most productive thing that could have happened. It means the field is honest about what the evidence actually shows. That's not failure. That's how a discipline earns the right to the next question.
